OpenAgData:
Agricultural research, searchable by where it happened

Every agricultural study has a location. A cover crop trial in Iowa produces different results than the same trial in Georgia. OpenAgData is built on a different premise: location is the primary axis, and everything else follows from it.

Saurav Das, PhD · Independent Researcher

Core mechanism

Map-first search

Every indexed study is pinned to the location where the research occurred — not where it was published.

Primary output

Place-relevant evidence

Secondary output

Research gap mapping

Funders and researchers see exactly where agricultural evidence is concentrated — and where it is absent.

SECTION I

The problem with how agricultural research is currently organized

Agricultural science produces an enormous volume of field-level evidence each year. The USDA, land-grant universities, and international research bodies collectively publish thousands of peer-reviewed studies annually on soil health, crop management, nutrient cycling, and climate adaptation. By any bibliometric measure, the knowledge exists.

The problem is structural, not scientific. Published research is organized by journal, discipline, and author — not by where it was conducted or who can use it. A Nebraska extension agronomist searching for cover crop evidence relevant to sandy loam soils in the Platte River Valley must manually compile studies from dozens of disconnected sources, filter by geography using crude keyword searches, and assess relevance without any spatial visualization. The same problem applies to USDA program officers writing conservation funding justifications, policy analysts building evidence briefs, and farmers trying to validate a practice change before committing acreage to it.

Scattered across hundreds of sources

Useful findings are buried in disconnected journals, agency portals, and institutional repositories. Most never reach practitioners at the moment of decision.

Geography treated as an afterthought

Existing tools index by topic, author, and date. What works in the Corn Belt may fail in the Southeast — but no tool makes that distinction systematic.

Research investment is invisible

No public tool shows where agricultural funding is spatially concentrated, making it impossible to systematically identify evidence gaps before they become policy failures.

SECTION II

What OpenAgData does differently

OpenAgData indexes agricultural research studies and maps them to their geographic origin — the actual field, county, or watershed where the work was conducted. Users navigate by map first: zoom into a region, and see what has been studied there. Every study is filterable by topic, practice, outcome type, and funding agency.

Before OpenAgData

  • Keyword search across disconnected journals
  • Manual geography filtering by state name in abstract
  • No visibility into research concentration or gaps
  • Weeks to compile a regional evidence brief
  • Published studies sit in journals, unreachable to practitioners

With OpenAgData

  • Map-first navigation — zoom to your region, see what exists
  • Studies indexed to actual research location, not publication origin
  • Funding agency and investment flow visualized spatially
  • Evidence brief compiled in minutes, not weeks
  • Open submission — researchers add their own work directly

SECTION IV

Why this problem is worth solving at infrastructure scale

The gap between what agricultural science knows and what farmers practice is not primarily a science problem. It is a knowledge infrastructure problem. The evidence exists. The distribution system is broken.

Estimates from USDA Economic Research Service and FAO consistently find that accelerating the adoption of evidence-based soil and crop management practices — even incrementally — would produce measurable gains in yield stability, input efficiency, and soil carbon storage at national scale. The bottleneck is not the research. It is the time and friction required to find it, assess its geographic relevance, and translate it into a decision.

OpenAgData removes that friction systematically. It does not replace peer review, extension services, or agronomic expertise — it makes all three more findable, faster, and spatially anchored to the actual geography of agricultural decision-making.

SECTION V

What makes this different from existing tools

vs. Google Scholar

Scholar indexes by text and citation; location is buried in abstracts. OpenAgData indexes by research origin as the primary axis, making geography a first-class search dimension.

vs. USDA portals

Agency portals are siloed by program and funding stream. OpenAgData aggregates across agencies and visualizes investment geography — something no single agency portal can do.

vs. AgriSearch tools

Most agricultural databases are subscription-gated and institution-facing. OpenAgData is open-access by design — any farmer or advisor can use it without credentials or cost.

vs. AI literature tools

AI summarization tools answer questions but cannot map evidence geographically or reveal where research is absent. OpenAgData is an infrastructure layer, not a summarization endpoint.

OpenAgData is early-stage infrastructure. The index is growing, the map is live, and the submission pipeline is open. The long-term vision is a platform where any farmer, advisor, or researcher can ask "what does the evidence say for my area?" and get a verified, spatially-grounded answer in seconds — not weeks.
